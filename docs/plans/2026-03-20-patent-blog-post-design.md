# Design: Patent Blog Post

## Title Options (A/B test candidates)

1. "Flock Safety Says They Don't Track Race. Their Patent Says Otherwise."
2. "Flock Safety Patented Racial Classification. SC Has No Law Against It."
3. "What Flock Safety Patented, and What It Means for South Carolina"

## Audience

Dual audience:

- **SC residents** who've heard the "it's just plates" talking point. The post debunks that claim using the patent as a smoking gun.
- **Local officials / council members** framed as "what did you actually approve?" Connects patent capabilities to what's in their Flock contracts.

## Central tension

Lead with the lie (Flock says "we don't identify race, gender, or ethnicity"), expand into the capability gap (what they can do vs. what they say they do), land on "your contract doesn't protect you from any of this."

## Relationship to existing posts

Builds on [Greenville Contracts](/blog/greenville-flock-contracts) without repeating its contract analysis. References the contract clauses (Section 2.12 unilateral upgrades, Section 4.5 perpetual data rights) and links back. No other existing post covers patents, FreeForm AI, or racial classification capabilities. Clean lane.

## Target length

2,500-3,000 words. Comparable to Greenville Contracts (the longest and strongest investigative piece on the site).

## SEO targets

- Primary: "Flock Safety patent," "Flock Safety racial classification," "Flock Safety facial recognition"
- Secondary: "do Flock cameras identify race," "Flock Safety patent facial recognition," "what do Flock cameras track"
- Long-tail FAQ: exact-match questions people search after hearing about Flock

## Structure

### Open: The contradiction (150 words)

Flock's product page quote ("we don't identify race, gender, or ethnicity") vs. patent US11416545B1 text (neural networks classifying gender, estimated height/weight, clothing, facial recognition data points). Matching individuals across multiple cameras using statistical similarity.

SC pivot: this company operates 1,000+ cameras in your state, feeding a database with 422 million reads, governed by zero laws.

**Source:** [Flock discrimination blog](https://www.flocksafety.com/blog/are-flock-products-discriminatory/), [Patent US11416545B1](https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/patent/US-11416545-B1)

### Section 1: What the patent actually says (400 words)

Quoted patent claims (not paraphrased). The specific language about race, gender, height, weight, clothing classification. Cross-camera tracking via statistical similarity. Facial recognition data points. Filing date (Oct 4, 2020), grant date (Aug 16, 2022), patent number for verifiability.

SC tie-in: every Flock camera in the state is hardware that could run this software. The patent describes what these specific devices are designed to do.

Pull quote: the patent's own language on racial classification, blockquoted.

**Source:** [Patent US11416545B1](https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/patent/US-11416545-B1)

### Section 2: What the cameras already do today (350 words)

FreeForm AI product capabilities: body type, clothing color, vehicle damage, roof racks, decals. Capability comparison table:

| Capability | Patent describes | FreeForm AI does today | Flock says publicly |
|---|---|---|---|
| License plate capture | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Vehicle make/model/color | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Body type / clothing | Yes | Yes | "Vehicle Fingerprint" |
| Height / weight estimation | Yes | No (public) | No mention |
| Gender classification | Yes | No (public) | "We don't identify gender" |
| Race / ethnicity classification | Yes | No (public) | "We don't identify race" |
| Facial recognition data points | Yes | No (public) | "We do not use facial recognition" |
| Cross-camera person tracking | Yes | No (public) | "Point-in-time images only" |

SC tie-in: FreeForm AI is already active on SC cameras. The gap between "what they do today" and "what they patented" is a software update, not a hardware change.

**Source:** [Flock FreeForm product page](https://www.flocksafety.com/products/flock-freeform), [Patent US11416545B1](https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/patent/US-11416545-B1)

### Section 3: Flock's own words (300 words)

The blog post where Flock admitted they have facial recognition capability but "choose not to use it." Product page says they don't use it. Patent says they built it. Blog says they could turn it on. Three contradictory positions from the same company.

SC tie-in: when a company holds three positions simultaneously, which one governs what happens to 422 million plate reads in SLED's database?

Pull quote (red strong): the three contradictory Flock statements, side by side.

**Source:** Flock "Future of Investigations" blog (verify URL), [Flock discrimination blog](https://www.flocksafety.com/blog/are-flock-products-discriminatory/), [Flock FreeForm product page](https://www.flocksafety.com/products/flock-freeform)

### Section 4: Your contract doesn't protect you (400 words)

Link to Greenville Contracts post. Pull the two critical clauses: Section 2.12 (unilateral upgrades: Flock can push upgrades "it deems necessary or useful" without asking) and Section 4.5 (perpetual data rights). The upgrade clause means Flock can activate patented capabilities without council approval, without public notice, without renegotiating.

SC tie-ins:
- Greenville's contract is representative of Flock contracts statewide
- Florence County's cameras on private property have even less oversight
- The Greenville sisters were pulled over at gunpoint from a plate misread. The patent describes a system that classifies people by race and could compound that kind of error with bias
- Plate misread accuracy issues (Business Insider) combined with racial classification = compounded harm

Pull quote (red strong): the unilateral upgrade clause language.

**Source:** [Greenville Contracts post](/blog/greenville-flock-contracts), [Business Insider misreads](https://www.businessinsider.com/flock-safety-alpr-cameras-misreads-2024), [WPDE Florence County](https://wpde.com/news/local/deputies-navigate-obstacles-to-put-up-more-security-cameras-in-florence-county-tj-joye-scdot-crime-flock)

### Section 5: 20 billion scans a month (300 words)

Scale context. Flock processes 20 billion plate scans monthly across its national network. ACLU analysis of how movement pattern data enables demographic profiling without explicit racial classification. Norfolk Carpenter ruling (Feb 2025): court found ALPR data requires a warrant because of the mosaic it creates. 500+ plate reads of a single vehicle in 4 months documented in Norfolk.

SC tie-in: SLED's database, with 3-year retention and 2,000+ users, is exactly the kind of data lake where patent capabilities become most dangerous. Cross-camera tracking across 99+ agencies means the system the patent describes already has the infrastructure to operate in SC.

**Source:** [ACLU analysis](https://www.aclu.org/news/national-security/surveillance-company-flock-now-using-ai-to-report-us-to-police-if-it-thinks-our-movement-patterns-are-suspicious), [Consumer Rights Wiki / Norfolk](https://consumerrights.wiki/w/Flock_license_plate_readers), [Post and Courier SLED data](https://www.postandcourier.com/news/alpr-cameras-south-carolina-flock-safety-license-plate-readers/article_787a262a-dbd2-11ee-a901-634acead588b.html)

### Section 6: What would stop this (250 words)

H4675's AI vehicle tracking ban would prohibit activation of patented capabilities. It's the only pending SC bill that addresses this. The other 3 bills regulate retention and access but don't touch what analysis Flock can run on the data.

SC tie-in: without H4675 or a local ordinance, there is no legal barrier between the patent and your cameras.

**Source:** [SC Legislature H4675](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/sess126_2025-2026/bills/4675.htm), [H4675 blog post](/blog/h4675-strongest-alpr-bill-in-sc)

1. **Local:** Ask your council: did you know your vendor patented racial classification? Has anyone on council read Patent US11416545B1? Does your contract allow unilateral upgrades? Find Your Rep button.
2. **State:** Support H4675, the only bill that would block this. Link to H4675 post.

## Voice

Per voice-dna.md:
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)
- Contractions always
- No em dashes (use commas, periods, colons, parentheses)
- Physical verbs for abstract processes
- No banned phrases (no "delve," "landscape," "it's worth noting," etc.)
- No "This isn't X. This is Y." pattern
- Bold sparingly, 1-2 key moments per section
- Parenthetical asides for editorial commentary
