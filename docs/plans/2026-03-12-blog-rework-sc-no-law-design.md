Rework the existing ~900-word post into a ~2,000-2,500-word deep-dive focused on documenting the legal vacuum, not the legislative fix. Four varied case studies illustrating different failure modes. Voice DNA applied throughout.

## Structure

### Opening Hook — ~200 words
- Lt. Ryan Terrell, North Charleston. June 2024. Used city-owned surveillance cameras to track his wife (suspected affair with fellow sergeant). Accessed feeds on multiple days. Followed her via live video. Confronted her and the sergeant in a Target parking lot. Demoted to Master Patrol Officer. Not fired.
- Pivot to Christy Hall quote: no public policy governs any of this. 200+ unpermitted Flock cameras discovered on state roads. Nobody at SCDOT knew they were there. That's the context Terrell operated in. That's the context every agency in SC operates in.

### The Database Nobody Authorized — ~300 words
- SLED centralized ALPR database. 422M reads 2019-2022. 100M+/year. 3-year retention. 2,000+ users. 99+ agencies. No statute authorizing it.
- Internal guidelines only, no penalties for violation.
- Fort Jackson and Parris Island as contributors.
- 2013 misconduct case: officer searched his own plate, falsified record. Access removed. SLED refused subsequent FOIA requests about misconduct, citing ongoing litigation.
- No way to know what's happened since. No law that would make any of it illegal.

### Berkeley County: 56 Cameras, Zero Debate — ~300 words
- Highest sheriff's office camera count in SC. More than 10x Richland County SO.
- No council vote, no news coverage, no transparency portal, no published use policy.
- Multiple other agencies in county (Goose Creek, Moncks Corner, Hanahan, Jamestown, St. Stephen) also on Flock network.
- Their state senator (Brian Adams, R) spoke in support of looser regulation during senate debate.
- What silent expansion looks like when no law requires public notice or approval.

### Florence County: The Private Property Workaround — ~300 words
- After SCDOT's permit pause (200+ unpermitted cameras on state roads), Sheriff Joye started installing cameras on private property to circumvent it.
- His framing: homeowners "wanna help any way they can."
- Florence PD's 30 cameras funded by $397K state legislative appropriation (not local budget).
- Local delegation introduced H5146 to force SCDOT to cooperate with Florence County on ALPR installation.
- Florence County SO retains data 1 year; Florence PD retains 30 days. Same county, different agencies, zero statewide standards.

### The Transparency Test — ~250 words
- Columbia Muckraker sent identical FOIA requests to multiple SC agencies for Flock audit logs (Dec 2025).
- Richland County SO: $9,152 quote. $37/hour search fee + $0.15/page. 6 weeks full-time work. 25% upfront.
- Florence PD: free, 1 day.
- Bluffton PD: USB drive, $20, days.
- Journalist argued fee violates SC FOIA law (copy charges don't apply to electronic records).
- Not the first time: Richland County ordered to pay $6,000 in FOIA penalties in 2020.
- If there's nothing to hide, why fight this hard to keep audit logs secret?

### What "No Law" Actually Means — ~300 words
- Updated consequences list, each bullet connected to a specific example from the post:
  - No limits on retention (SLED keeps data 3 years, no legal basis)
  - No access controls (2,000+ users across 99+ agencies)
  - No installation authorization required (200+ unpermitted cameras, Florence private property workaround)
  - No penalties for misuse (Terrell demoted, not fired; 2013 SLED falsification, access removed)
  - No transparency requirements (Richland County's $9,152 FOIA wall)
  - No democratic oversight (Berkeley's 56 cameras with zero public debate)
- The absence of law isn't theoretical. Every case study in this post happened because of it.
- End on the weight of all these examples converging: none of this is illegal, because no law exists to make it illegal.
