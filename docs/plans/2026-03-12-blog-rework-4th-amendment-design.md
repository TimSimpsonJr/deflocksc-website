## Structure

### Section 1: The Promise (2012-2018) — ~250 words
- United States v. Jones (2012): GPS tracking requires a warrant.
- Carpenter v. United States (2018): warrantless cell-site location data access violates 4th Amendment.
- Court recognized aggregated location tracking reveals "privacies of life."
- Two rulings, same principle: government can't build a map of your movements without judicial oversight.
- For a few years, it looked settled.

### Section 2: The Workaround (2018-2024) — ~300 words
- Carpenter's constraint only applies when government compels data handover.
- Flock Safety's model: cities buy cameras, data enters shared cloud network, government buys subscription access.
- Third-party doctrine background (brief): courts historically allowed access to voluntarily shared data. Carpenter was supposed to erode this. The private-company middleman model sidesteps the erosion.
- Establishes the loophole mechanically. No stats yet, just structure.
- Tone: explaining a scam to a friend.

### Section 3: The Doors Open (2025) — ~500 words
- UWCHR report: 3-door framework (front, back, side door).
- Front door: explicit local authorization for federal access.
- Back door: federal access without local knowledge. 10+ agencies in Washington compromised.
- Side door: local officer runs search on behalf of federal agent. 4,000+ documented. Nearly impossible to prevent.
- Colorado: 25 departments in secret pilot. CEO denial on camera, written admission 3 weeks later.
- Framing: not about any specific agency. The system has no guardrails. If one agency can get in, any can.
- 20+ cities terminated/suspended contracts (Denver, Cambridge, Evanston).
- Greenville Sisters case as parallel: ALPR harm at local level too. Held at gunpoint from misidentification.
- Let facts carry the weight. Don't editorialize the Colorado sequence.

### Section 4: The Scale (present day) — ~300 words
- San Jose: 261,000+ warrantless queries in 14 months.
- Virginia: ~3,000 federal-agency-related searches in one year.
- South Carolina: SLED database, 422M plate reads 2019-2022, 100M+/year, 3-year retention, 2,000+ users, 99+ agencies, military contributors.
- Mosaic theory callback: individual scans harmless, aggregated = church, doctor, rally, protest, support group.
- Supreme Court recognized this pattern as protected in Carpenter. Happening anyway.
- No state statute authorizing any of it. No retention limits, access controls, warrant requirement, or penalties.
- Let numbers land. Short paragraphs.

### Section 5: Fighting Back — ~350 words
- IJ v. City of Norfolk: first major federal ALPR lawsuit under 4th Amendment. Allowed to proceed. But litigation takes years.
- H.4675 specifics, showing how each provision closes a gap from the post:
  - Bans third-party cloud storage (kills the back door)
  - Requires warrants for data access (applies Carpenter principle)
  - 21-day retention limit (vs. SLED's 3-year no-limit)
  - Bans AI-based vehicle tracking
  - Quarterly independent audits (SC Inspector General)
  - Civil remedies (individuals can sue, damages + attorney's fees)
  - Illegally obtained data inadmissible
  - Existing contracts voided on passage
- Sponsored by 4 Freedom Caucus Republicans. Brief bipartisan angle.
- Link to H4675 deep-dive post.
- End on tension: Carpenter established the principle 8 years ago. H.4675 would enforce it. Sitting in committee.
