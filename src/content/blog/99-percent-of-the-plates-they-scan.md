An LAPD audit found that over 99% of license plates scanned by their ALPR cameras belonged to people with no connection to any crime. Not suspects. Not persons of interest. Just people driving.

That number holds everywhere these cameras operate. In South Carolina, SLED's database collected **422 million license plate reads** between 2019 and 2022. Over 100 million per year. The overwhelming majority of those reads are you, your neighbors, your family, going about normal life.

Every one of those reads is retained for **3 years** and accessible to **2,000+ individual users** across **99+ agencies**.

## What "scanning every plate" actually means

Flock Safety cameras don't target specific vehicles. They photograph every plate that passes, cross-reference it against databases in real time, and log the result regardless of whether there's a match. Every plate. Every time.

Over weeks and months, that creates a detailed record of your movements. Not a snapshot, a pattern. Where you go every morning. Whether you visit a particular neighborhood regularly. When you deviate from your routine.

The Supreme Court recognized the significance of this in Carpenter v. United States (2018): aggregated location data reveals the "privacies of life." Individual data points might be harmless. Stitched together, they tell a story most people consider deeply private.

## Your family's movements, on file

Think about what a year of plate scans reveals about a family:

Your kid's school, every morning and afternoon. The pediatrician. The orthodontist. Weekend sports leagues. Your church on Sunday. Your spouse's doctor on Thursday. The route you take home from work. The friend you visit on the other side of town. Whether someone in your household showed up at a political rally, a gun show, a support group meeting, a custody attorney's office.

None of this requires anyone to be suspected of a crime. The cameras record all of it, for everyone, automatically.

In South Carolina, Flock cameras are now operating near **100+ school systems** nationwide. The 74 reported in February 2026 that ICE has accessed data from cameras originally installed for student safety. Cameras sold to protect kids are being used to track their parents.

## The chilling effect

Legal scholars call it a "chilling effect": when people know they're being watched, they change their behavior. They skip the political meeting. They don't go to the protest. They think twice about the support group. They avoid the mosque, the synagogue, the nontraditional church.

These are all constitutionally protected activities. Freedom of religion. Freedom of assembly. Freedom of association. And a government database that logs who showed up at each of these places, searchable by 2,000+ law enforcement users, doesn't need to be actively misused to cause harm. The knowledge that it exists is enough.

Rep. Todd Rutherford (D-Richland) has been pushing ALPR regulation in South Carolina since 2020. He highlighted a case where an ALPR system was used to track a domestic partner, turning surveillance technology into a stalking tool. The system doesn't care whether the person with access has good intentions.

## "But it helps catch criminals"

Sure. So would putting a GPS tracker on every car in the state. The question is whether we're comfortable with the tradeoff.

Right now, South Carolina has no law governing how long plate scans are kept (SLED retains for 3 years), who can access them (2,000+ users across 99+ agencies), whether federal agencies can query them (they can), or what happens if someone misuses the data (nothing, legally).

In 2013, a SLED officer used the ALPR database to search for his own vehicle and falsified the record. His access was removed. No criminal charges. No statute made it illegal.

The argument for these cameras assumes the system works as advertised, that only authorized people access it for authorized purposes, that nobody abuses it, and that the data is properly secured. South Carolina's track record doesn't support that assumption.

## What would change

Four bills in the SC Legislature would set basic guardrails:

**H. 4675** is the strongest. It would ban Flock's cloud storage entirely, prohibit AI-based vehicle tracking, set a 21-day retention limit, and block immigration enforcement access. Sponsored by 4 Freedom Caucus Republicans.

**S. 447** and **H. 3155** require visual confirmation before ALPR-based traffic stops, limit retention to 90 days (down from SLED's current 3 years), prohibit selling data, and create misdemeanor penalties for violations.

**H. 4013** offers similar protections with broader co-sponsor support.

These aren't radical proposals. They're the minimum: tell people how long you're keeping their data, limit who can see it, and create consequences if someone misuses it. That's the bar. South Carolina hasn't cleared it yet.

If your rep sits on Judiciary or Education & Public Works, they decide whether these bills move.
