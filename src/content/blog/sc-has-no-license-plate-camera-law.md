In February 2024, South Carolina's Transportation Secretary Christy Hall told the Post and Courier something that should've made headlines: "There has not been public policy put into place by the South Carolina Legislature regarding the use of these devices, (or the) privacy concerns regarding the collection, handling and disposition of the data they collect."

She was talking about the **200+ Flock Safety cameras** her agency had just discovered on state roads. Installed without permits. Nobody at SCDOT knew they were there.

That's the situation in South Carolina right now. Hundreds of cameras scanning license plates on public roads, feeding data into a statewide database, and not a single law governing any of it.

## What's actually happening with your data

SLED (the South Carolina Law Enforcement Division) operates a centralized ALPR database that's been collecting plate scans since at least 2019. The numbers: **422 million license plate reads** between 2019 and 2022. Over **100 million per year**. That data is retained for **3 years** and accessible to **99+ agencies** through **2,000+ individual user accounts**.

There's no statute authorizing this database. SLED's own guidelines are internal policy, not law. They carry no penalties if violated.

Flock Safety, the company behind most of these cameras, tells cities their data is "permanently deleted after 30 days." That's true for Flock's own servers. But the same data gets piped to SLED's database, where it sits for 3 years. Flock's 30-day promise is a technicality. The data lives on.

Fort Jackson and Parris Island are listed as contributors to the SLED database. Military installations, feeding plate scan data into a system with no legal oversight. That's where we are.

## How Greenville got 57 cameras without a vote

Greenville PD's initial Flock contract ($22,000 for 11 cameras) was funded through **federal civil asset forfeiture funds**. That's a back-channel funding route that bypassed normal city council appropriations. No public vote. No budget line item for residents to see and question.

The system has since grown to at least 57 cameras under GPD alone, with 88+ across Greenville County when you count the Sheriff's Office, Greer, Mauldin, Simpsonville, and Travelers Rest. GPD won't disclose where the cameras are, citing concerns about "compromising effectiveness."

The city also runs SafeWatch, a program that asks residents to register their Ring and Nest cameras so police can request footage on demand. And Greenville's most recent camera RFP specifically required compatibility with neighborhood ALPR technology. The city is deliberately designing its surveillance network to integrate with private cameras. At least one HOA is already operating its own ALPR system.

## What the Legislature hasn't done

Four bills are sitting in committee right now:

**H. 4675** (House, Judiciary Committee since January 2026) is the strongest. It bans third-party cloud storage of camera data, prohibits AI-based vehicle tracking beyond license plates, sets a 21-day retention limit, requires warrants for data access, and explicitly bans immigration enforcement use. If passed, every existing Flock contract in SC would be void. It's sponsored by 4 SC Freedom Caucus Republicans.

**S. 447** (Senate, Judiciary Committee since March 2025) and **H. 3155** (House, Judiciary Committee since January 2025) offer solid protections: visual confirmation required before ALPR-based traffic stops, 90-day data retention limits, prohibition on selling data, misdemeanor penalties for violations, and mandatory written oversight policies.

**H. 4013** (House, Education & Public Works Committee since February 2025) includes similar protections with 5 co-sponsors, suggesting broader support across districts.

Three of the four bills (S. 447, H. 3155, H. 4013) share the same gap: **none of them restrict federal agency access to SC ALPR data**. H. 4675 closes that gap with its cloud storage ban and immigration enforcement prohibition, but even it is sitting untouched in committee.

Rep. Todd Rutherford (D-Richland) has been pushing ALPR regulation for years. He introduced similar bills in 2023, 2021, and as far back as 2020. His framing: "This is no slippery slope. This is the hill, and we're sliding down it."

And this has bipartisan support. The late Rep. Garry Smith (R-Simpsonville), a 19-year Republican veteran of the SC House and ALEC state chairman, publicly backed ALPR regulation in 2020: "There is a lack of accountability at this point in time. We need to set some reasonable limitations on that."

Smith retired in 2022. His quotes are still in print. His seat is now held by Rep. David Vaughan (R), who has no known position on the issue and sits on the Education & Public Works Committee where H. 4013 is pending.

## What "no law" actually means

Without a statute, there are no enforceable limits on:

- How long your plate scan data is retained (SLED keeps it 3 years)
- Who can access it (2,000+ users across 99+ agencies)
- Whether federal agencies can query it (they can)
- What happens if someone misuses it (nothing, legally)
- Whether cameras can be installed on public roads without permission (200+ were)

In 2013, a SLED officer used the ALPR database to search for a vehicle he personally owned, found his plate, and **falsified the record** to show someone else's plate. His access was removed. When the Post and Courier filed a FOIA request asking for more recent misconduct cases, SLED refused to provide any documents, citing ongoing litigation.

That's the accountability mechanism for a database with 422 million records. One documented case. No way to know what's happened since. And no law that would make any of it illegal.

## Four bills. Three committees. Zero movement.

The SC Public Interest Foundation is suing SLED over the constitutionality of the database. Greenville resident John Sloan is a named plaintiff. But lawsuits take years.

The faster path is legislative. Four bills, bipartisan support (Freedom Caucus Republicans on H. 4675, Democrats on H. 3155), documented harms, and a state transportation secretary on record saying the Legislature hasn't acted. The pieces are there. They're just sitting in committee.

If your rep sits on Judiciary or Education & Public Works, they have a direct say in whether these bills move.
