On July 30, 2025, Flock Safety CEO Garrett Langley went on 9NEWS in Colorado and said his company had no federal contracts. Three weeks later, the same station reported that Flock had been secretly giving CBP (Customs and Border Protection) a Flock account since May 2025, using it to send data-sharing invitations to local police departments across the country. 25 Colorado departments accepted without being told what they were signing up for.

By August 25, Langley issued a written statement that tried to split the difference: Flock doesn't have a "contractual relationship" with DHS agencies, but it has "engaged in limited pilots" with CBP and HSI.

That's the company South Carolina has been handing its license plate data to. Here's the full picture.

## The CEO's own words

Langley has a pattern. When public pressure builds, he responds not with transparency but with escalation.

In December 2025, after residents in Staunton, Virginia raised concerns about Flock cameras at a public meeting, Langley sent an unsolicited email to the police chief characterizing the opposition as people who want to "defund the police, weaken public safety, and normalize lawlessness."

Police Chief Jim Williams, a 41-year law enforcement veteran, publicly rejected that framing. He called the residents' questions "democracy in action." The city terminated its Flock contract weeks later.

Langley's email tells you how the company views public oversight: as an attack.

## The Colorado timeline

This is the clearest documented case of deception in Flock's public record:

**May 2025:** Flock gives CBP a Flock account to send 1:1 data-sharing invitations to local police departments nationwide. No public announcement. No notification to the departments receiving the invitations.

**July 30, 2025:** Langley appears on 9NEWS and says, "We don't have any federal contracts."

**August 19, 2025:** 9NEWS reports that 25 Colorado departments had been sharing data with CBP through this secret program.

**August 25, 2025:** Langley issues a written statement admitting to "limited pilots" with CBP and HSI.

Same month, Flock confirmed it "cannot monitor how CBP uses the system or ensure searches comply with state restrictions." In Colorado, sharing personal identifying information for immigration enforcement is illegal.

## The Illinois audit

In August 2025, an Illinois Secretary of State audit found that Flock had violated state law by allowing federal agencies to access local ALPR data without authorization. 12 local agencies' data had been accessed by CBP.

During the fallout, Langley admitted that Flock lacked proper safeguards for federal data sharing, was running unapproved pilot programs, and that the company's own leadership was unaware of immigration-related searches in their system.

The CEO of the company tracking your movements admitted he didn't know who was accessing your data.

Evanston, Illinois terminated its Flock contract over this. So did multiple other cities.

## Cameras that won't turn off

When cities try to leave, Flock makes it difficult. In Eugene, Oregon, cameras were reactivated after the city ordered them shut down. In Cambridge, Massachusetts, same thing. Evanston, Illinois reported the same pattern.

The Record documented multiple cases where Flock cameras remained active after cities had explicitly asked for them to be turned off. These aren't isolated incidents. They're a pattern.

## The cancellation wave

The deception has consequences. Denver (Flock's own showcase city) terminated in February 2026 after discovering 1,400+ immigration-related searches and Flock's refusal to cooperate with auditors. Mountain View, California turned off all cameras that same month after discovering Flock had enabled nationwide federal access. Lynnwood, Washington; Los Altos Hills, California; Santa Cruz, California; Staunton, Virginia; Evanston, Illinois: all terminated.

These aren't anti-police cities making symbolic gestures. Denver was Flock's poster child. Staunton's police chief is a 41-year veteran. They cancelled because the company lied to them.

## What this means for South Carolina

South Carolina currently has **200+ Flock cameras** operating on public roads. The state has no statute governing ALPR data, no retention limits, no access controls, no penalties for misuse.

Flock tells SC cities their data is "permanently deleted after 30 days." That's true for Flock's own servers. But the same data gets piped to the SLED database, where it sits for **3 years**, accessible to **2,000+ users** across **99+ agencies**.

The company that ran a secret federal pilot program without telling its own customers, whose CEO denied federal contracts on camera 3 weeks before being publicly exposed, whose cameras kept running after cities ordered them shut down: that's the company managing the surveillance infrastructure for communities across South Carolina.

Three bills are sitting in committee right now that would set basic rules for this technology: retention limits, access controls, penalties for misuse. None of them have moved.

If your rep sits on Judiciary or Education & Public Works, they have a direct say in whether these bills advance.
