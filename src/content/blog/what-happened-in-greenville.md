Two sisters were driving in Greenville when a Flock Safety camera misread their rental car's license plate and flagged it as stolen. Police pulled them over at gunpoint. They were handcuffed. The car wasn't stolen. It had been improperly reported. They're now suing Greenville PD.

That's the most visible thing that's gone wrong. It's not the only thing.

## 88 cameras, no public vote

Greenville County has at least **88 Flock Safety cameras** spread across multiple agencies: 57 under Greenville PD, 11 under the County Sheriff's Office, plus cameras in Greer, Mauldin, Simpsonville, and Travelers Rest. Furman University PD has them too. At least one HOA is running its own ALPR system.

The initial Greenville PD contract ($22,000 for 11 cameras) was funded through **federal civil asset forfeiture funds**. That's money seized from people, often without conviction, routed through a federal program and spent without normal city council appropriations. No public vote. No budget line item for residents to see.

Since then, the system has grown from 11 cameras to 57 under GPD alone. The city's most recent camera RFP specifically required compatibility with neighborhood ALPR technology, which tells you the plan: integrate private cameras into the police network. SafeWatch, GPD's voluntary camera registry, already asks residents to register their Ring and Nest cameras so police can request footage on demand.

GPD won't say where the cameras are. Their stated reason: disclosing locations would "compromise effectiveness."

## The SLED database

Every plate scan from these cameras doesn't just sit in Greenville. The data flows to the SLED (South Carolina Law Enforcement Division) database, where it's retained for **3 years** and accessible to **2,000+ users** across **99+ agencies**.

Flock tells cities their data is deleted after 30 days. That's technically true for Flock's own servers. But SLED keeps it for 36 months. The 30-day claim is a technicality. Your data lives on.

The SLED database has been running since at least 2019. The numbers: **422 million license plate reads** between 2019 and 2022. Over 100 million per year. No state statute authorizes this database. SLED's own guidelines are internal policy, not law, and carry no penalties for violations.

## The SLED insider

In 2013, a SLED officer used the ALPR database to search for a vehicle he personally owned. He found his own plate in the system and **falsified the record** to show someone else's plate instead.

His access was removed. That's the extent of the consequences.

When the Post and Courier filed a FOIA request asking for more recent misconduct cases, SLED refused to provide any documents, citing ongoing litigation. One documented abuse case. No way to know how many more have happened since. And no law that would make any of it illegal.

## What happened next door

Spartanburg County sits just up I-85 from Greenville. The former Spartanburg County Sheriff, Chuck Wright, oversaw Flock camera deployments during his tenure. Wright was a prominent local figure with a long career in law enforcement.

He pled guilty to federal charges: conspiracy to commit theft of federal funds, conspiracy to commit wire fraud, and obtaining controlled substances through fraud. He faces up to 30 years in prison. His name was removed from the Sheriff's Office sign in May 2025.

A convicted criminal had unsupervised access to surveillance tools that track where residents drive. That's what "no oversight" looks like in practice.

## The lawsuit

The SC Public Interest Foundation is suing SLED over the constitutionality of the database. Greenville resident John Sloan is a named plaintiff. But lawsuits take years.

The faster path is legislative. Three bills are sitting in committee right now (S. 447, H. 3155, H. 4013) that would set retention limits, require access controls, and create penalties for misuse. They have bipartisan support. They haven't moved.

Greenville County has 88+ cameras, no public disclosure of locations, a funding mechanism that bypassed voters, a statewide database with no legal authority, documented insider abuse, and a neighboring county's sheriff convicted of federal crimes while controlling the same technology.

If your rep sits on Judiciary or Education & Public Works, they have a say in whether the pending bills move.
